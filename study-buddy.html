Overview

Study Buddy is an app designed to improve the experience and effectiveness of studying for students. In this project I conducted independent research on 34+ students and in following, collaborated within a group of UX designers to iteratively develop the app.

Problem Space

Students face a myriad of challenges in their everyday life, however, one particularly detrimental challenge is focus. Through research we found that students were shown to encounter numerous distractions such as their phone, their attention span, a desire to have a social life, and other factorsthat impaired their study abilities. Through research and analysis, I investigated the nature of focusand study effectiveness and produced requirements that will assist in creating a solution that initiatesimprovements in this experience for students.

Literary Review

Pychyl et al. (2000) analysed the nature of procrastination through a 5 day study on 45 undergraduate students. Through this, they found a relationship between negative emo-tions such as frustration and resentment, and procrastination. This produced a flow-oneffect where students found it even harder to cope with the tasks, due to the anticipa-tion of more negative emotions. In consequence, students delayed the task and woulddistract themselves with feel-good stimuli. This was consistent with interviews conductedin assignment one by Team Interaction Design Kings.Similarly, Ozer, Demir, and Ferrari (2013) conducted cognitive intervention on 10 stu-dents for 5 weeks aimed at reducing procrastination. The researchers conducted multiplesessions with the students, aimed at developing an understanding around their own pro-crastination habits, and irrational beliefs regarding procrastination (these might include- I only work well afterXtime). The research "indicated that helping clients changeirrational beliefs is an effective way to decrease procrastination." (p.133). This suggeststhat changing the mindset around procrastination, could be keystone in improving focus.

In continuation, Cirillo (2006) investigated the pomodoro technique, a technique in whichusers were able to improve their work efficiency, by breaking their work into 25 minutechunks followed by 5 minute breaks. After three pomodoros, the participant is given a long30 minute break instead. Cirillo found this lead to more lucid conscious work and higherproductivity. Breaking up work is further supported by a study conducted by Ariely andWertenbroch (2002) that found that self-imposed deadlines were more effectively whenthey were evenly spaced (i.e.broken up).

By reflecting on past research, insights on what constitutes a good solution may be gained.In his article, Chen (2007, pp. 31–32) discusses the potential for users to experience flow,which is a state of ’complete and energised focus in an activity’. Using examples from gaming, surfing, and design, Chen’s premise is that flow ultimately creates a more engag-ing experience for users which in turns translates to better outcomes both for developersand users. Chen suggests that by offering choices embedded within the core activities, achallenging experience, and providing clear goals that user engagement and effectivenesscan be increased. Additionally, Deneke et al. (2017) investigate the interaction design(IxD) process, through the development of a medical robotic agent. Through this, theycultivate enhanced understanding of important characteristics that improve user expe-rience. Deneke et al. synthesises research from Wainer et al. (2006), and Lee et al.(2012) to suggest that physically embodied agents were preferred over virtual or remoteagents (p.54). Additionally, by referencing Kriglstein and Wallner (2005), Deneke et al.suggest that robots with human features were more enjoyable, and enhanced emotionalengagement in users.
